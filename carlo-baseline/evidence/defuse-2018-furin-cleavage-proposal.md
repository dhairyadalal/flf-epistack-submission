# The 2018 DEFUSE proposal: a pre-pandemic blueprint to insert furin cleavage sites into bat SARS-related coronaviruses

## Claim
In early 2018 a consortium led by EcoHealth Alliance (Peter Daszak), with the University of North Carolina (Ralph Baric), the Wuhan Institute of Virology (Zhengli Shi), and Duke-NUS (Linfa Wang), submitted a grant proposal named DEFUSE to DARPA's PREEMPT program. The proposal described building full-length infectious clones of bat SARS-related coronaviruses and inserting "human-specific" proteolytic (furin) cleavage sites into spike proteins, with some experimental work to be performed at relatively low biosafety. DARPA rejected the proposal; the document was leaked publicly in September 2021.

## Key facts
- The proposal was titled "DEFUSE" (Defusing the Threat of Bat-borne Coronaviruses), submitted to DARPA's PREEMPT (PREventing EMerging Pathogenic Threats) program in early 2018, with a requested budget of roughly $14 million. It was rejected by DARPA. (The Intercept 2021; DRASTIC 2021–22)
- Core engineering task as worded in the proposal: "We will introduce appropriate human-specific cleavage sites and evaluate growth potential in [mammalian cell types] and HAE cultures." (Quoted directly by The Intercept from the leaked document.)
- SARS-CoV-2's furin cleavage site sits at the S1/S2 junction of the spike. Microbiologist Richard Ebright (Rutgers) stresses that SARS-CoV-2 "is the only virus in its entire genus of SARS-related coronaviruses that contains a fully functional cleavage site at the S1, S2 junction." The DEFUSE plan to insert such a site therefore maps onto SARS-CoV-2's most anomalous feature. (The Intercept 2021)
- The proposal involved WIV: "The Wuhan Institute of Virology team will continue to collect biodiversity surveys from SARSr-CoV viruses of bat caves across S. China." Bulk genetic-engineering work was nominally slated for UNC in North Carolina, but critics note materials and protocols could be transferred. (USRTK; The Intercept 2021)
- Biosafety concern: reporting and DRASTIC analysis note the proposal envisioned coronavirus work at relatively low biosafety, and critics emphasized the risk that some work could be performed in Wuhan at BSL-2, lower than the level the danger warranted. This BSL-2 point is the most contested factual detail and is reconstructed from the leaked document plus commentary rather than from a single unambiguous proposal sentence in the news coverage I verified.
- Daszak did not dispute the document's authenticity when it surfaced in September 2021. (The Intercept 2021)

## Evidence quality
This is documentary evidence of high authenticity but indirect probative value. The proposal is a real primary document, leaked by the DRASTIC research collective and reported by The Intercept (an established outlet) in September 2021; its authenticity was not disputed by EcoHealth's president. So the claim "this proposal existed and said these things" is very well supported. What it does NOT establish is that the proposed work was ever performed: DARPA rejected the proposal, and there is no direct evidence the rejected experiments were carried out (in the US, in Wuhan, or anywhere). The document proves pre-pandemic *intent and capability* to engineer exactly the kind of feature SARS-CoV-2 has — which is circumstantially striking — but intent is not execution.

## Strongest counterargument
Several points cut against treating DEFUSE as strong origin evidence:
- **Rejection, no demonstrated execution.** DARPA declined to fund DEFUSE. There is no released evidence that the furin-site insertion work was performed, so the document cannot show that SARS-CoV-2 was made this way.
- **Andersen's S2′ objection.** Kristian Andersen (a natural-origin proponent and lead author of "The Proximal Origin of SARS-CoV-2") has argued DEFUSE is largely irrelevant to origins because, on his reading, the proposal's cleavage-site language concerns a different site (the S2′ position) and proteolytic cleavage sites can be activated by various enzymes, not specifically furin at S1/S2. (This is contested; the proposal does explicitly reference furin and the S1/S2 region, so the dispute is partly interpretive.)
- **Furin sites can arise naturally.** Furin cleavage sites occur across other coronaviruses (e.g., some betacoronaviruses, MERS lineage, HKU1) and can emerge via natural recombination or in-host evolution. So the existence of the feature in SARS-CoV-2 does not by itself require engineering, and a documented plan to insert one does not prove insertion occurred.

## Confidence assessment
- **Claim confidence:** 0.95 — The existence and contents of the DEFUSE proposal are very well documented (leaked primary document, reported by The Intercept, authenticity not disputed by EcoHealth). The 0.05 discount is mainly for the BSL-2/low-biosafety specifics, where the exact proposal wording is harder to pin down than the furin/cleavage-site language, and for interpretive disputes (e.g., S2′ vs S1/S2) about what precisely was meant.
- **Support strength:** moderate (for lab-leak) — A documented, detailed, pre-pandemic plan by a consortium including WIV to insert furin/human-specific cleavage sites into bat SARS-related CoVs is a genuinely notable coincidence given SARS-CoV-2's uniquely anomalous furin site. It raises the prior that lab manipulation was plausible and available. But because the proposal was rejected and no execution is demonstrated, it is suggestive circumstantial/documentary evidence, not proof — hence moderate rather than strong.
- **Net:** A calibrated reasoner should treat DEFUSE as strong evidence of pre-pandemic intent and capability to build SARS-CoV-2-like features, which meaningfully raises the lab-origin hypothesis, while remembering that a rejected, unexecuted blueprint is not evidence the virus was actually engineered.
