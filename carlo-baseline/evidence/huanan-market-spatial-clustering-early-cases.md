# Earliest December 2019 cases cluster tightly around the Huanan market

## Claim
The residential locations of the earliest known December 2019 COVID-19 cases in Wuhan — including those with no reported epidemiological link to the Huanan Seafood Wholesale Market — were geographically centered on that market, with the spatial pattern dispersing across the city by January–February 2020. Worobey et al. argue this centering would be extremely unlikely unless the outbreak began at or very near the market.

## Key facts
- In Worobey et al. (Science 2022), among 155 December 2019 cases mapped, the median distance to the Huanan market was ~4.28 km, and the center-point of cases fell ~1.02 km from the market (p≈0.007 vs. a population-density null). [Worobey 2022]
- Strikingly, the 120 cases with *no reported link* to the market were located, on average, *closer* to it than the market-linked cases (center-point ~0.91 km from market, p≈0.006; unlinked vs. linked p≈0.029). [Worobey 2022]
- The market fell inside the highest-density 1% contour of a kernel-density estimate for both all cases and the unlinked subset. [Worobey 2022]
- The December clustering contrasts with January–mid-February 2020, when cases were dispersed across Wuhan — the expected signature of an outbreak seeded at a single point source and then spreading. [Worobey 2022]
- The Wuhan Institute of Virology (~12 km from the market, across the Yangtze) is not near the December case centroid. [Worobey 2022]
- After critics (Stoyan & Chiu 2024) argued the market lies outside the 95% confidence region of the centroid, Débarre & Worobey (arXiv:2403.05859, 2024) re-ran the critics' own method and reported the mode falling "at the entrance of a parking lot at the market itself," with the market inside the 95% confidence region.

## Evidence quality
The primary source is peer-reviewed (Science, 2022), uses pre-registered-style spatial statistics (kernel density estimation, center-point/centroid distance tests against population-density and age-matched nulls, spatial relative-risk surfaces), and has generated an unusually detailed public methodological debate — a sign of scrutiny, not of weakness. The case-location data, however, are not raw line-lists released by Chinese authorities; they were reconstructed from a WHO-China joint mission report and published figures, so the underlying geocoding has limited independence and cannot be fully audited. The temporal pattern (December clustering → January dispersal) is robust and intuitive. The specific claim that unlinked cases cluster *even more tightly* than linked cases is the load-bearing and most contested element.

## Strongest counterargument
The most serious rebuttal is **ascertainment bias**, not raw clustering. Critics (Stoyan & Chiu 2024 in JRSS-A; D. Weissman 2024) argue: (1) any contagious disease produces clustered case maps, so rejecting a population-density null shows *clustering exists*, not that the market *caused* it; and (2) early cases may have been preferentially *detected* because Chinese investigators, alerted to the market, searched for market-connected pneumonia — making "December cases near the market" partly a product of where authorities looked. Weissman specifically flagged the finding that unlinked cases sit closer than linked cases as a red flag for "major proximity ascertainment bias." A broader version of this critique (Bloom and others) holds that the December case set is too small, too curated by Chinese authorities who suppressed and selected data, to bear the inferential weight placed on it. Débarre & Worobey (arXiv:2405.08040, 2024) reply that the unlinked-vs-linked pattern is explained by infections occurring away from home plus stochasticity, not bias, and that broad community surveillance for "viral pneumonia of unknown cause" in early 2020 was not market-targeted. This dispute is genuinely unresolved: if the data were ascertainment-biased toward the market, the spatial result is partly circular.

## Confidence assessment
- **Claim confidence:** 0.8 — That the *available, reconstructed* December 2019 case locations are statistically centered on the market is well established and survived a re-analysis using the critics' own method. The residual ~20% reflects the unauditable, Chinese-curated provenance of the case set and the live possibility of ascertainment bias inflating the apparent centering.
- **Support strength:** moderate — Strong *if* the case sample is representative and not ascertainment-biased; the bias objection is the reason it is not "strong." It locates the December epicenter at a live-animal market and not at the WIV, but a market epicenter is also compatible with an infected person (e.g., a lab worker) seeding the market — spatial centering identifies *where* sustained transmission first concentrated, not the ultimate upstream source.
- **Net:** A calibrated reasoner should treat the spatial centering on the market as real and meaningful evidence for a market-origin, while holding open that ascertainment bias and curated Chinese data could partly manufacture the pattern, and that "epicenter of spread" is not strictly identical to "site of the index spillover."
