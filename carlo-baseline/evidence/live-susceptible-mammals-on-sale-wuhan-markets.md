# Live SARS-CoV-2-susceptible mammals were documented on sale in Wuhan markets, including Huanan, before the pandemic

## Claim
Systematic monthly surveys of Wuhan wet markets from May 2017 to November 2019 documented 47,381 live wild animals from 38 species (18 of them mammals) on sale in poor hygienic and welfare conditions, including raccoon dogs, masked palm civets, and American mink — species known to be susceptible to SARS-like coronaviruses. Seven shops at the Huanan Seafood Market were among those selling live mammals. No bats or pangolins were observed in the trade.

## Key facts
- The survey was conducted by Xiao et al. (2021) in *Scientific Reports* and covered May 2017–Nov 2019, with monthly visits to four market complexes (Huanan Seafood, Baishazhou, Dijiao Pet, Qiyimen Live Animal), spanning 17 shops selling live wildlife [Source 1].
- Total of **47,381 individual animals from 38 species** were recorded for sale; **18 were mammalian species**, including raccoon dog (*Nyctereutes procyonoides*, ~38 individuals/month), masked palm civet (*Paguma larvata*, ~11/month), and American mink (*Neovison vison*, ~10/month) [Source 1].
- Raccoon dogs and masked palm civets were directly implicated in the 2003 SARS-CoV-1 epidemic as intermediate/amplifying hosts; raccoon dogs, civets, and mink are all experimentally and/or naturally susceptible to SARS-CoV-2 [Source 1].
- **No pangolins and no bats** were observed in the surveyed trade, which the authors note undercuts the earlier hypothesis of direct pangolin or bat sale at the market as the spillover route [Source 1].
- Conditions were poor: "almost all animals were sold alive, caged, stacked"; ~30% of examined mammals bore gunshot/trap wounds; none of the 17 shops posted origin or quarantine certificates [Source 1].
- The Huanan market specifically: the paper reports **7 shops there selling live mammals**, with photographic documentation — directly contradicting the WHO–China joint report's statement that no live mammal sales at Huanan were verified [Source 1, Source 2].
- Independent corroboration that susceptible mammals were physically present at Huanan: metagenomic analysis of CCDC environmental swabs found raccoon dog and other susceptible-mammal genetic material in SARS-CoV-2-positive samples, especially in the market's southwest corner wildlife stalls (Crits-Christoph/Débarre et al.) [Source 3].

## Evidence quality
The core claim rests on a **peer-reviewed paper** (*Scientific Reports*, an open-access Nature journal) built on a pre-existing, independently motivated dataset: the surveys were originally undertaken to study a tick-borne disease (SFTSV) and zoonotic risk, not the pandemic, reducing the risk of post-hoc bias. The animal counts are direct field observations by the authors. This is strong, replicable documentation of *what was traded*. It is corroborated on the specific point of Huanan mammal sales by later genomic environmental sampling (Source 3) and by social-media and vendor photographs compiled by other groups.

Crucial limitation in the evidence itself: the surveys **did not test any of these animals for SARS-CoV-2** (the virus did not yet exist in circulation for most of the period, and no archived market animals were ever virologically tested by authorities). So the paper establishes the *susceptible-host supply chain*, not a positive virus-in-animal result.

## Strongest counterargument
The paper shows susceptible animals were *present and sold*, but it does **not** show that any were infected, nor that infection at the market was the spillover event rather than a later human-driven superspreading amplification. Two specific rebuttals:

1. **No positive animal sample exists.** As the IDSociety/Science Speaks commentary stresses, China never disclosed SARS-CoV-2 test results for live market animals, and no infected intermediate host has ever been found — for SARS-CoV-2 the animal link remains inferential, unlike SARS-CoV-1 where infected civets were recovered [Source 2].
2. **The raccoon-dog DNA correlation is contested.** Jesse Bloom's reanalysis found that across the full CCDC dataset, raccoon-dog genetic material did not robustly correlate with viral abundance and was if anything somewhat more associated with virus-*negative* samples; the CCDC made the same point. The presence of susceptible mammals and the presence of virus in the same building does not by itself establish that the mammals were the infection source rather than infected humans contaminating animal stalls [Source 3 includes both the positive analysis and the critiques].

A lab-leak proponent would add that a market selling susceptible mammals is fully expected in any large Chinese city and would exist regardless of how the virus actually originated, so its explanatory weight for *origin* is limited.

## Confidence assessment
- **Claim confidence:** 0.93 — The factual claim (these species, in these numbers, sold in these conditions, including at Huanan) is well-documented in a peer-reviewed paper using a pre-pandemic dataset and is corroborated independently. The small residual uncertainty reflects reliance on a single survey team for the counts and a known discrepancy with the WHO–China report (which I judge the WHO report got wrong, not Xiao et al.).
- **Support strength:** moderate — for the **natural-origin** hypothesis. It robustly establishes the *necessary precondition* for market zoonosis (a sustained supply of stressed, susceptible mammals in human contact at the epicenter) and refocuses attention onto plausible intermediates (raccoon dogs/civets) rather than bats/pangolins. But "necessary precondition present" is weaker than "spillover demonstrated"; no infected animal was found, so it does not close the loop.
- **Net:** A calibrated reasoner should treat this as solid, near-certain documentation that the zoonotic *opportunity* existed at the exact epicenter — meaningfully raising the prior on a market spillover — while recognizing it is a precondition, not proof, and that the absence of any tested-positive animal leaves the natural-origin chain inferential.
