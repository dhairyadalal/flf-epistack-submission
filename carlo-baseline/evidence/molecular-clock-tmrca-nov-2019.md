# Molecular-clock dating places SARS-CoV-2's common ancestor in Nov–Dec 2019, with no genomic sign of long cryptic human circulation

## Claim
Bayesian molecular-clock phylodynamics place the time of the most recent common ancestor (tMRCA) of sequenced SARS-CoV-2 genomes in roughly mid-November to mid-December 2019, with the inferred index case / first human infections in the weeks just before (plausibly mid-October to mid-November 2019). The sampled genomic diversity is shallow and shows no robust signal of a long, deep tail of cryptic human transmission predating the Wuhan outbreak — a pattern consistent with a recent introduction near the outbreak epicenter.

## Key facts
- **Pekar et al. 2021 (Science)** combined coalescent molecular-clock analysis of 583 early Chinese genomes with forward epidemic simulation (BEAST + FAVITES). They estimated a tMRCA with mean ~9 December 2019 (and place the index-case infection on or around early-to-mid November; primary analysis ~4 Nov, 95% upper HPD ~15 Oct). Crucially, they note over two-thirds of simulated epidemics seeded by a single introduction went **extinct**, implying very little room for a long pre-pandemic transmission chain.
- The same authors stress an important caveat: **the tMRCA is not the same as the moment of zoonosis or the first human infection.** Coalescent pruning of basal lineages can push the tMRCA *forward* in time from the true index case by days to weeks. The tMRCA is therefore a lower bound on "how old the sampled tree is," not a direct date of spillover.
- **Nie et al. 2020 (Virus Research, PMC7366979)**, an independent analysis of 112 early genomes, estimated a tMRCA of **12 November 2019 (95% BCI: 11 Oct – 9 Dec 2019)** and an evolutionary rate ~9.9 × 10⁻⁴ subs/site/year — convergent with multiple other early-pandemic estimates clustering around late November 2019.
- **Pekar et al. 2022 (Science, abp8337)** refined the picture: the pre-February-2020 diversity comprises two lineages (A and B) whose tMRCAs fall in a window with 95% HPDs spanning roughly **13 November – 23 December 2019**, interpreted as two separate introductions at/near the Huanan market within weeks of each other.
- Across these analyses, no widely accepted phylodynamic result recovers a deep, well-supported pre-October-2019 human transmission tree; estimates converge on a narrow late-2019 window tightly tied in time to the Wuhan outbreak.

## Evidence quality
This is **peer-reviewed, genomic/phylodynamic** evidence, replicated across independent groups (Pekar/Wertheim/Worobey; Nie et al.; and broader reviews) using standard Bayesian coalescent methods. That convergence is a genuine strength. Important limitations: (1) early-pandemic datasets carried weak temporal signal, so rate and tMRCA estimates were sensitive to clock-model choice and sampling; (2) molecular-clock dating tells us about the **sampled** genetic diversity, not directly about zoonosis timing or about lineages that were never sequenced; and (3) the most influential elaborations (Pekar 2021/2022) come from an overlapping author group, so they are not fully independent of one another. The claim "no long cryptic human circulation" is an *inference* from shallow diversity, not a direct observation.

## Strongest counterargument
The headline rebuttal is that **a recent, shallow tMRCA does not by itself discriminate natural spillover from a lab origin**, and may even understate how early the virus was circulating. Kumar, Pond and colleagues (*Molecular Biology and Evolution*, 2021) used mutation-order ("progenitor") methods and argued the SARS-CoV-2 progenitor was likely circulating **6–8 weeks before Wuhan-1**, i.e., into late October or earlier — implying the tMRCA postdates real circulation by a substantial margin. More fundamentally, a lab-origin scenario does **not** predict a long human transmission tail either: an engineered or cultured virus released shortly before the outbreak would *also* produce a shallow, recent tMRCA near Wuhan. So the recent common ancestor is consistent with both hypotheses; it argues against only the specific sub-claim of *prolonged undetected human spread*, not against lab origin as such. Additionally, the two-lineage / two-introduction model (Pekar 2022) is contested — some authors argue lineage A and B can be linked by intermediate haplotypes or a single introduction plus early mutation, which would weaken the "two independent zoonoses" framing while leaving the dating intact.

## Confidence assessment
- **Claim confidence:** 0.85 — That phylodynamic dating robustly places the sampled common ancestor in roughly Nov–Dec 2019 is well replicated and uncontested in its broad strokes. The narrower assertion of "no cryptic prior circulation" is softer: it is an inference from shallow diversity and is actively disputed by progenitor-based analyses, so I keep confidence below the level I'd assign the bare dating result.
- **Support strength:** moderate (for natural origin) — The recent tMRCA constrains origin scenarios to a narrow window tied to Wuhan in late 2019, which is broadly more comfortable for a single recent spillover than for a long-circulating lab-adapted lineage. But because a recent lab release predicts the *same* shallow tree, this evidence does not cleanly separate the two hypotheses. Its real weight comes when combined with the spatial clustering and market-linkage evidence, not on its own.
- **Net:** A calibrated reasoner should treat the late-2019 tMRCA as solid and as ruling out *prolonged* prior human circulation, while recognizing it is only weakly diagnostic between natural and lab origin on its own, and that the exact date of zoonosis sits somewhat earlier than the tMRCA.
