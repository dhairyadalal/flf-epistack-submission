# Pervasive natural recombination in sarbecoviruses, and a SARS-CoV-2 lineage that diverged decades before 2019

## Claim
Recombination-aware phylogenetics shows that bat sarbecoviruses recombine extensively in nature (Lytras et al. detected ~160 recombination events across 78 genomes), and that the SARS-CoV-2 lineage diverged from its closest sampled relatives (RaTG13, RmYN02, pangolin and Southeast-Asian viruses) decades to over a century ago. The receptor-binding domain (RBD) features that allow human-ACE2 binding appear to be inherited from a deep common ancestor rather than recently acquired, and SARS-CoV-2 is not a recombinant of any virus sampled to date.

## Key facts
- **Frequent natural recombination is established.** Lytras et al. (2022) detected **160 unique recombination events** across **78 sarbecovirus genomes**, with ~21 major breakpoints and recombination hotspots clustered near the spike gene, but recombination *coldspots* within the RBD and NTD of spike and in ORF8 (Lytras et al. 2022, *Genome Biology and Evolution*).
- **Multi-decade-to-century divergence from sampled relatives.** Boni et al. (2020) estimated the divergence of the SARS-CoV-2 lineage from its bat reservoir at roughly 1948, 1969, or 1982 depending on the dataset and how recombinant regions are masked, concluding the lineage "has been circulating unnoticed in bats for decades." Lytras et al. estimate common ancestors with RmYN02 ~40 years ago, RaTG13 ~50 years ago, and with the Cambodian (RShSTT182/200, ~1907) and Thai (RacCS203, ~1883) viruses more than a century ago.
- **RBD/receptor-binding motif appears ancestral, not a recent recombinant.** Boni et al. found the receptor-binding motif important for human-ACE2 specificity is "an ancestral trait shared with bat viruses and not one acquired recently via recombination," and that SARS-CoV-2 is not a recombinant of any sarbecovirus detected to date. A later Bayesian recombination analysis (Esquivel Gomez et al. 2024, *Scientific Reports*) similarly concluded the variable loop of the RBD did **not** have a recombinant origin and that the key ACE2-binding residues were likely present in the common ancestor of these viruses.
- **Mechanistic implication.** Because co-circulating bat sarbecoviruses recombine readily, novel spike/RBD combinations can be generated in nature without human intervention, and no sampled lab strain is a plausible direct progenitor given the decades of divergence.

## Evidence quality
High. Three independent, peer-reviewed analyses by partly non-overlapping research groups converge: Boni et al. 2020 (*Nature Microbiology*, an influential, heavily cited study including Andrew Rambaut and David Robertson), Lytras et al. 2022 (*Genome Biology and Evolution*), and Esquivel Gomez et al. 2024 (*Scientific Reports*, a Max Planck group using a distinct Bayesian recombination-detection method). The recombination signal in sarbecoviruses is a robust, replicated finding; the divergence-time estimates carry wide confidence intervals (e.g., Boni's 95% HPDs span roughly 1879–2009) because molecular-clock dating of recombining RNA viruses is genuinely uncertain, but all estimates place divergence from sampled relatives well before 2019.

## Strongest counterargument
The most important rebuttal is that this evidence is **necessary but not sufficient** to exclude a lab origin, and it sidesteps the single feature most central to that debate: the **furin (polybasic) cleavage site (FCS)** at the S1/S2 junction, which is unique to SARS-CoV-2 and absent from all other known sarbecoviruses. Segreto & Deigin (2021, *BioEssays*, "The genetic structure of SARS-CoV-2 does not rule out a laboratory origin") argue that (a) showing the RBD is ancestral does nothing to explain the FCS, whose natural origin "has proved elusive," and (b) natural recombination and engineered chimeras can produce indistinguishable genetic structures, so demonstrating that recombination *can* generate novel spikes does not show that *this* virus arose that way rather than via passage or construction in a lab. In short: that the *RBD* is inherited is compatible with both hypotheses, and the *FCS* — the part lab-leak proponents most emphasize — is exactly the region these recombination analyses do not explain. (A partial counter-rebuttal: later work shows FCS-like sites can arise naturally in related bat coronaviruses by single nucleotide changes — e.g. Lytras-adjacent and *Communications Biology* 2022 work on European SARS-related bat CoVs — so the FCS gap is narrowing but not closed.)

## Confidence assessment
- **Claim confidence:** 0.9 — The core factual claims (pervasive natural recombination; ~160 events/78 genomes; pre-2019 divergence from sampled relatives; ancestral rather than recently-recombined RBD) are well-replicated and peer-reviewed. I dock confidence mainly because divergence-time point estimates are model-dependent with wide intervals, not because the qualitative claim is in doubt.
- **Support strength:** moderate — for natural origin. It strongly establishes a *plausible natural mechanism* and rules out any *sampled* lab strain as a direct ancestor, which weakens crude "RaTG13 was tweaked" framings. But it does not address the FCS, does not exclude an *unsampled* progenitor that could equally have been collected and manipulated, and a deep natural divergence of the *backbone* is consistent with several origin scenarios. It is evidence about mechanism availability more than about what actually happened in Wuhan.
- **Net:** A calibrated reasoner should treat this as solid evidence that the SARS-CoV-2 genomic backbone and RBD are products of long natural evolution — removing one class of lab-origin arguments — while noting it neither explains the furin cleavage site nor, by itself, settles the origin question.
