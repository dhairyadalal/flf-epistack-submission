# No close progenitor virus or infected intermediate host has been found years after emergence

## Claim
More than four years after the emergence of SARS-CoV-2, no direct animal progenitor virus has been sampled and no infected non-human animal of any species (intermediate host or reservoir) has been found that links to the pandemic, despite substantial sampling. The closest known relatives are bat viruses (~96–96.8% identity) whose shared ancestor with SARS-CoV-2 dates back several decades.

## Key facts
- **The closest known relatives are still distant.** RaTG13 (Yunnan bats) is ~96% identical to SARS-CoV-2 — about 4% / ~1,150 nucleotide differences, implying decades of divergence. BANAL-52/-103/-236 (Rhinolophus bats, Feuang, Laos, ~530 km from the Yunnan site) reach ~96.8% identity, the highest of any known virus, but their common ancestor with SARS-CoV-2 is still estimated at decades back (Temmam et al. 2022, Nature).
- **No bat or intermediate host carrying SARS-CoV-2 itself has ever been found.** Holmes et al. (2021, Cell) state plainly that "no animal reservoir or intermediate host for SARS-CoV-2 has yet been identified," attributing this to undersampling and/or low prevalence rather than to absence of a natural host.
- **The molecular-epidemiology (market) hypothesis itself requires unsampled animal hosts.** Pekar et al. (2022, Science) argue for two zoonotic introductions at the Huanan market; their model requires an unsampled single-mutation A/B intermediate to have circulated in animals — i.e., the animal source is inferred, not observed.
- **Market environmental sampling found susceptible-species DNA but no infected animal.** Liu et al. (2023, Nature) and Crits-Christoph/Débarre et al. (2024, Cell) found DNA of raccoon dogs, civets and other susceptible species co-located with SARS-CoV-2 RNA in Huanan market stall samples. This places susceptible animals at the plausible epicenter, but **no animal sample has tested positive for SARS-CoV-2 infection**; co-mingled DNA and viral RNA does not establish that any animal was infected.
- **Contrast with SARS-CoV-1.** For the 2002–2003 outbreak, masked palm civets at live-animal markets were found carrying virus ~99.8% identical to human SARS-CoV within months (Guan et al. 2003, Science; CDC EID 2005), and during the 2003–2004 re-emergence patient sequences were essentially identical to civet sequences — a direct, infected intermediate host with a near-perfect genetic match. No comparable finding exists for SARS-CoV-2.

## Evidence quality
The underlying factual claim is strong and largely uncontested: it rests on peer-reviewed work across the spectrum of the origins debate (Holmes et al. 2021 Cell; Temmam et al. 2022 Nature; Pekar et al. 2022 Science; Liu et al. 2023 Nature; Crits-Christoph et al. 2024 Cell). Even authors who argue strongly for a natural market origin concede that the direct progenitor and any infected intermediate remain unsampled. The dispute is not over the fact but over its interpretation — whether prolonged failure to find an infected animal favors a lab origin or merely reflects sampling limits.

## Strongest counterargument
"Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence," and the inference from this fact to a lab origin is weak for several converging reasons:

1. **Sampling has been limited and politically constrained.** The wildlife supply chains feeding Wuhan markets (farms in southern China, Laos, Vietnam) were largely culled and not systematically tested early; the live animals present at Huanan in late 2019 were removed before sampling. Holmes et al. (2021) and the 2024 Cell study emphasize that the relevant animals were never sampled in the relevant window.
2. **Natural spillovers frequently lack an identified intermediate.** No intermediate host was ever conclusively identified for MERS-related lineages before camels, for Ebola, or for some early SARS-CoV-1 introductions; the SARS-1 civet result, while fast, was aided by an ongoing live-animal trade that was still operating when sampling occurred.
3. **The market DNA evidence is positively suggestive.** Susceptible species (raccoon dogs, civets) demonstrably present at the exact stalls with the most SARS-CoV-2-positive environmental samples is what a zoonotic spillover would predict, even absent a positive animal test.
4. **The decades-of-divergence gap exists on every hypothesis.** Because the closest known viruses are ~3–4% divergent, no sampled virus is a plausible direct ancestor under *any* origin scenario — including a lab one. The absence of a close progenitor therefore does not specifically favor lab leak; it reflects how little of the bat-coronavirus reservoir has been sequenced (Holmes et al. 2021; Temmam et al. 2022). Liu et al. (2023, Nature, "Surveillance of SARS-CoV-2 at the Huanan Seafood Market") and Crits-Christoph et al. (2024, Cell) present this evidence as supporting, not undermining, a market zoonotic origin.

## Confidence assessment
- **Claim confidence:** 0.92 — As stated (no sampled direct progenitor; no confirmed infected animal of any species linked to the pandemic), the claim is essentially accurate and agreed across the literature. The small uncertainty reflects ongoing debate about whether the market environmental/DNA evidence should count as a partial identification of the source.
- **Support strength:** weak — IF true (which it is), it moves the origin question only modestly toward lab-leak. The decades-long divergence gap exists under all hypotheses, intermediate hosts are often unfound in genuine zoonoses, and sampling has been demonstrably incomplete and politically obstructed. The SARS-1 contrast is real but rests on a single unusually favorable precedent.
- **Net:** A calibrated reasoner should treat the missing progenitor/intermediate as a genuine and somewhat surprising gap that is *weakly* consistent with a non-zoonotic introduction, while recognizing it is far better explained by undersampling of a vast, poorly characterized bat reservoir and disrupted wildlife supply chains — and that the same gap would exist even if the origin were natural.
