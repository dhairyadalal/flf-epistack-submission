# The RBD was not "optimal" for human ACE2 at emergence

> Written by Claude (not by Carlo). One item in the COVID-origins evidence catalog.

## Claim
At the time SARS-CoV-2 emerged, its receptor-binding domain (RBD) bound human ACE2 efficiently but by a structural solution **not predicted to be optimal** by the computational/structural models then available. Andersen et al. (2020) argued this is more consistent with natural selection than with deliberate engineering, because a designer using those models would have chosen a different, "ideal" binding configuration. Subsequent variants that *improved* ACE2 affinity show the original RBD was not at a fitness peak.

## Key facts
- Andersen, Rambaut, Lipkin, Holmes & Garry (2020, *Nature Medicine*, "The proximal origin of SARS-CoV-2") found the SARS-CoV-2 RBD bound human ACE2 with high affinity but via residues that **differed from those predicted to be optimal** by the structural modeling that had been used to study SARS-CoV-1 ACE2 binding. Their inference: a deliberate engineer would likely have used a known-optimal backbone (e.g. the SARS-CoV-1 RBD), so the unexpected solution points to natural selection.
- The natural binding solution **exists in nature**: pangolin-associated coronaviruses share 5 of the 6 key ACE2-contact residues with SARS-CoV-2 (Lam et al. 2020, *Nature*; Xiao et al. 2020, *Nature*) — see `pangolin-cov-rbd-five-contact-residues`. This removes the need to posit design to explain the RBD.
- Deep mutational scanning of the RBD (Starr et al. 2020, *Cell*) and the later emergence of higher-affinity variants (Alpha N501Y; Delta; Omicron) demonstrate empirically that the **ancestral RBD was not affinity-maximized** — natural selection subsequently found tighter-binding configurations, exactly as expected if the original arose by chance-plus-selection rather than by optimization.

## Evidence quality
Peer-reviewed and partly replicated. The pangolin RBD homology and the post-emergence affinity gains are robust, independently established facts. The core "not optimal" inference is a **reasoning argument layered on the 2020 structural literature**, not a direct experiment, so its strength depends on how good those 2020 models were — a point critics press (below).

## Strongest counterargument
The argument is weaker than it first appears, for two reasons. (1) "Not optimal by 2020 computational models" assumes a designer would rely on, and trust, those models; an engineer using **directed evolution or passage** (rather than rational design) would *also* produce a non-"optimal," natural-looking RBD — so non-optimality does not cleanly distinguish natural selection from lab passage (see `serial-passage-cell-culture-adaptation`). (2) The same authors who made this argument privately expressed serious doubts about a natural origin around the time of writing (`proximal-origin-private-doubts-controversy`), which does not refute the RBD science but cautions against treating the paper as a settled, disinterested verdict. The RBD point argues against *rational design specifically*, not against all lab scenarios.

## Confidence assessment
- **Claim confidence:** 0.8 — The factual core (RBD not affinity-optimal at emergence; natural binding solution present in pangolin CoVs; later variants bind tighter) is well supported. The interpretive "not what an engineer would build" layer is sound but model-dependent.
- **Support strength:** weak — It effectively rebuts the **rational-design / bioweapon** sub-hypothesis, but does little against the "leak of a natural or passaged virus" variants of lab origin, which is where the serious lab-leak case now sits.
- **Net:** Good evidence that SARS-CoV-2 was not rationally engineered for human ACE2 binding, but only a weak discriminator between natural spillover and a leak of an ancestrally natural virus.
