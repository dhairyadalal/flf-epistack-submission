# Two early lineages (A and B) and the inference of two separate zoonotic spillovers at the Huanan market

## Claim
Early SARS-CoV-2 diversity comprised two distinct lineages, A and B, differing by exactly two nucleotides (positions 8782 and 28144). Pekar et al. (2022) argue from phylodynamic simulation that these two lineages were introduced into humans by at least two *separate* spillover events around mid-to-late November 2019, both geographically and temporally linked to the Huanan Seafood Wholesale Market — the pattern expected from repeated animal-to-human transmission at a live-wildlife market rather than from a single point-source release.

## Key facts
- **The two lineages are real and minimally separated.** Lineages A and B differ by exactly two nucleotide substitutions, at genome positions 8782 and 28144 (Pekar et al. 2022). This part of the claim — the existence and definition of A and B — is essentially uncontested.
- **Both lineages were detected at the market.** Environmental samples from the Huanan market and early cases include both lineage A and lineage B viruses (Worobey et al. 2022; Pekar et al. 2022, 2025).
- **Inferred timing.** Pekar et al. estimate the lineage B primary infection around 18 November 2019 (95% HDI 23 Oct–8 Dec) and lineage A roughly a week later, ~25 November 2019 (95% HDI 29 Oct–14 Dec).
- **The "two introductions" inference is a simulation/Bayesian result, not a direct observation.** Pekar et al. simulated epidemic phylogenies and asked how often a single introduction would produce the observed two-lineage topology with no sampled intermediate haplotypes. Originally they reported Bayes factors ~60 favoring two introductions over one.
- **A 2023 erratum substantially lowered the headline number.** Pekar et al. issued an erratum (Science 2023, doi:10.1126/science.adl0585) correcting coding errors that had *overestimated* the Bayes factors. After correction the BF dropped to roughly 4 — still nominally favoring two introductions, but far weaker than originally claimed.
- **The "intermediate genome" defense.** A key requirement for the two-introduction inference is that no genuine A–B intermediate haplotype existed in early humans. Pekar et al. (2025, Virus Evolution) re-examined two Shanghai genomes (SH-P37-2, SH-P55-2; "T/T" haplotype) that Lv et al. (2024) proposed as intermediates, and argued that, given their February 2020 sampling dates, they are far more likely *derived* (P of being a true intermediate ≈ 0.002) than transitional.

## Evidence quality
Peer-reviewed (Science, Virus Evolution), from a well-resourced group (Pekar, Worobey, Lemey, Andersen, Rambaut and colleagues). The genomic facts (two lineages, two-nucleotide difference, both lineages at the market) are solid and independently verifiable. The *inferential* layer — that this proves two separate spillovers — rests on a specific phylodynamic model with debatable assumptions and has been formally corrected once (2023 erratum) and repeatedly contested. It is also entangled with the Worobey spatial paper, which has its own erratum (2024, doi:10.1126/science.adp1133) and is disputed by Stoyan & Chiu. So: high-quality raw data, contested model-dependent conclusion.

## Strongest counterargument
Critics argue the two-introduction inference is an artefact of how the hypotheses were tested, not a robust signal:
- McCowan (2025, arXiv:2502.20076) argues the test was *imbalanced*: the single-introduction model was burdened with extra constraints (minimum clade-size ratio, ≥2 mutations between clade roots, specific root topology) that the two-introduction model escaped. Applying symmetric conditions, he reports the corrected BF moves from ~4 to roughly 0.5–0.25 — i.e. *against* two introductions, or at best inconclusive.
- The 2023 erratum already cut the original BF from ~60 to ~4, conceding the headline statistic was inflated by code errors. A BF of 4 is "substantial" but not "strong" evidence by conventional Bayesian thresholds.
- Lv et al. (2024) and others contend that A–B intermediate genomes may have existed, which would collapse the two-introduction requirement; Pekar et al.'s 2025 rebuttal is itself contested rather than settled.
- A single introduction at or near the market (e.g. of an unsampled recent common ancestor that quickly split into A and B) is not excluded and would equally explain co-location of both lineages at Huanan. The two-lineage pattern is therefore *consistent with* but does not *uniquely require* two animal spillovers.

Note that even the strongest critics (McCowan, Stoyan & Chiu) are attacking the *statistical inference of two spillovers*, not arguing for a lab origin; defeating this argument leaves the broader market-centrality question open rather than supporting lab leak.

## Confidence assessment
- **Claim confidence:** 0.85 — that two lineages A and B exist, differ by two nucleotides, and that Pekar et al. published an inference of two separate Huanan-linked introductions is certainly true (≈1.0). I set 0.85 because the *substantive* claim ("phylodynamic analysis argues both were introduced separately") remains the authors' position even after the erratum and the 2025 intermediate-genome paper — but the strength of that argument is materially weaker than the original 2022 framing, and serious peer critiques (post-erratum BF ~4, McCowan's reanalysis) are unrebutted.
- **Support strength:** moderate — for natural origin. Two near-simultaneous, market-centered introductions fit a reservoir of infected traded animals better than a single lab release, which most parsimoniously seeds one lineage. But the inference is model-dependent and contested; a single introduction that split into A and B near the market is not excluded, so this is suggestive rather than decisive.
- **Net:** A calibrated reasoner should treat the two-lineage *fact* as solid and mildly favoring a market-based zoonotic spillover, while heavily discounting the once-dramatic Bayes factor: after the erratum and McCowan's critique, the quantitative case for *two* distinct spillovers (versus one introduction that diversified) is weak-to-moderate, not the "slam dunk" sometimes portrayed.
