# WIV bat coronavirus sequence database taken offline in September 2019

## Claim
The Wuhan Institute of Virology's (WIV) main bat- and rodent-virus sample/sequence database (hosted at `csdata.org` / `batvirus.whiov.ac.cn`), which held tens of thousands of sample records and a body of unpublished bat coronavirus sequences, became inaccessible from outside the institute around September 12, 2019 — roughly three months before the COVID-19 outbreak was publicly acknowledged. The full underlying data has never been released to independent investigators.

## Key facts
- The database is a real, documented resource: "Wildlife-borne viral pathogen database," developed under WIV/Shi Zhengli's group, catalto­guing bat and rodent virus samples collected across China (and later Laos). Sources describe it as covering on the order of 22,000 sample/sequence records, of which a protected, access-controlled portion held unpublished sequences (the "100+ unpublished bat coronavirus sequences" figure cited by the Washington Post editorial board). (DRASTIC/Demaneuf report; Washington Post editorial)
- The principal claim about the offline date traces to **Gilles Demaneuf and Billy Bostickson (DRASTIC), "An investigation into the WIV databases that were taken offline" (Feb 19, 2021)**, which used the Internet Archive's Wayback Machine and other web-history records to date the loss of external accessibility to **September 12, 2019**. (Demaneuf/Bostickson 2021)
- This date — predating the publicly acknowledged outbreak by ~3 months — was repeated as established by the **Washington Post editorial board (Feb 5, 2021)**, which stated the database "was accessible until Sept. 12, 2019, when it became unreachable" from outside WIV. (Washington Post)
- WIV/Shi Zhengli's explanation: the database was removed for **cybersecurity reasons** during the period of intense pandemic attention. Via Peter Daszak, Shi reported on the order of "3,000 hacking attempts" against WIV systems; Shi told the BBC the institute's website and staff emails had been attacked. In a January 26, 2021 email she said the database was taken offline "during [the] Covid-19 pandemic." (BBC; Daszak remarks; Demaneuf 2021)
- The WHO joint mission (Feb 2021) was told by Chinese officials that the database had been "removed after repeated hacking attempts," but the mission did not obtain or audit the raw underlying data. (BuzzFeed News / Aldhous)
- A **separate, related episode** sometimes conflated with this one: 13 early-pandemic SARS-CoV-2 sequences were uploaded to the NIH Sequence Read Archive in March 2020 and deleted in June 2020 (recovered by Jesse Bloom from cloud backups). That is a different dataset from the offline WIV bat-virus database. (Bloom 2021; Aldhous)

## Evidence quality
The **factual core** — that the WIV bat-virus database stopped being externally accessible in or around September 2019 and has not been released — is well supported. It rests on web-archive forensics (a replicable method using public Wayback Machine snapshots) by DRASTIC, and the September 12, 2019 dating was accepted and republished by mainstream outlets (Washington Post editorial board; BuzzFeed News science reporting by Peter Aldhous) and surfaced in official channels (European Parliament written question E-000608/2022; the WHO mission was briefed on the removal). Chinese officials and Shi Zhengli have **not disputed that the database was taken offline** — they dispute the interpretation.

The originating analysis is from DRASTIC, an open-source investigative collective, not a peer-reviewed venue, and the report is self-published (Zenodo/ResearchGate). However, the underlying datum (a site becoming unreachable on a given date) is independently checkable, and the offline status is uncontested. So this is **strong on the bare fact, weak as a self-published analytic narrative** about motive.

The **interpretation** (that the timing implies concealment of a SARS-CoV-2 progenitor) is circumstantial and unproven. No source demonstrates that the hidden sequences contain a closer relative of SARS-CoV-2.

## Strongest counterargument
1. **The offline date is not as anomalous as it looks.** The database in question was a research-data portal that had received limited maintenance; web resources of this kind frequently lapse, change hosts, or restrict access for mundane reasons. A snapshot gap from September 2019 establishes when *external accessibility via that interface* dropped — not that the institute deliberately "scrubbed" anything in response to an outbreak that had not yet been recognized. Critics of the lab-leak framing (and fact-checks of the "deleted to hide COVID" narrative) note that nothing in the web-archive record shows the *content* was destroyed, only that a public-facing portal became unreachable.
2. **Shi Zhengli's cybersecurity explanation is plausible.** WIV did face an extraordinary surge of attention and hostility in 2020. Taking a research database offline to limit exposure during that period is an ordinary defensive move. The genuine **tension** is that the *earliest* loss of accessibility (Sept 2019) predates the pandemic, which her "during the pandemic" framing does not cleanly explain — but a portal can go offline in Sept 2019 for routine reasons and *separately* be kept offline later for security, and the two need not share a cause.
3. **Non-disclosure is consistent with many motives**, including legitimate ones (proprietary unpublished data, state secrecy reflexes, fear of being scapegoated) that have nothing to do with hiding a progenitor virus. Absence of the data cannot, by itself, distinguish "they are hiding a smoking gun" from "they are stonewalling an investigation they find threatening regardless of guilt."

## Confidence assessment
- **Claim confidence:** 0.9 — That the database became externally inaccessible around September 12, 2019 and remains undisclosed is well corroborated and undisputed by WIV itself; only minor uncertainty attaches to the exact date and to the precise scope/content figures (the "100+ unpublished sequences" vs "~22,000 records" describe different layers and are sometimes loosely reported).
- **Support strength:** weak — for the lab-leak hypothesis. The timing is genuinely suggestive and the continued non-disclosure is a real obstruction to investigation, which legitimately raises suspicion. But it is fully explainable by innocent causes, contains no demonstrated link to SARS-CoV-2's actual ancestor, and an offline web portal is not evidence of what the underlying data show. It shifts priors only modestly.
- **Net:** A calibrated reasoner should treat this as a real and frustrating transparency failure that mildly favors the lab-leak hypothesis through obstruction and suspicious timing, while recognizing that "we cannot see the data" is not the same as "the data are incriminating," and that the September 2019 date is suggestive rather than probative.
