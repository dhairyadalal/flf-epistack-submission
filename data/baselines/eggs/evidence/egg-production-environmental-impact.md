# Egg production has environmental impacts that vary by system and comparison

## Claim
An environmental reading of “good to eat” evaluates greenhouse-gas emissions, land use, feed production, manure, eutrophication, and other impacts; results depend on production system, functional unit, geography, and the food used as a comparator.

## Key facts
- Large comparative life-cycle datasets place eggs below several livestock products on some impacts but above many plant proteins.
- Egg-production LCAs identify feed production and manure management as important contributors.
- Housing systems can trade environmental efficiency against welfare-relevant characteristics, so “better” depends on the selected outcomes and weights.

## Evidence quality
Life-cycle assessment is the appropriate general method, but estimates are model-dependent. System boundaries, allocation choices, feed assumptions, geography, and the unit of comparison can materially change results.

## Strongest counterargument
Comparative global averages can obscure local production practices and nutritional substitution. A per-kilogram, per-protein, per-calorie, and per-serving comparison can rank foods differently, so an environmental verdict without a declared functional unit is underspecified.

## Confidence assessment
No numeric confidence is assigned. The robust conclusion is structural: environmental assessment requires a different source universe and outcome definition than either health or welfare assessment.
