# Individual lipid responses to dietary cholesterol from eggs vary

## Claim
Individuals differ in their serum-lipid response to dietary cholesterol from eggs, so a population-average association does not fully answer whether a particular person will experience a clinically relevant change.

## Key facts
- Controlled feeding studies have reported substantial between-person variability and have described hypo- and hyper-responsive participants.
- Reviews propose differences in cholesterol absorption, synthesis, metabolic status, and genetics as possible contributors.
- A change in a lipid biomarker is not identical to a demonstrated change in long-term cardiovascular events.

## Evidence quality
Controlled feeding studies support the existence of heterogeneous biomarker responses, but many are small, short, and use egg doses above ordinary consumption. Definitions of a “hyper-responder” vary, and long-term clinical relevance remains less certain than the short-term lipid response.

## Strongest counterargument
The heterogeneity may have limited clinical importance if LDL and HDL change together, if effects remain within healthy ranges, or if the observed response does not translate into different cardiovascular outcomes. Small studies can also exaggerate apparent responder subgroups.

## Confidence assessment
No numeric confidence is assigned. The evidence supports splitting a single population-level claim into a population warrant and a person-specific uncertainty statement; it does not support diagnosing an individual from the literature alone.
