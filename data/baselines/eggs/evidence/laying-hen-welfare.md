# Egg production creates husbandry-dependent laying-hen welfare harms

## Claim
Whether eggs are “good to eat” can reasonably include the welfare of laying hens; welfare outcomes depend on housing and management and include risks such as bone lesions, restriction of behavior, group stress, and other husbandry-related harms.

## Key facts
- EFSA's 2023 scientific opinion identified multiple highly relevant welfare consequences for laying hens.
- EFSA recommended avoiding cages and addressing space, density, lighting, litter, and behavioral needs.
- Different systems trade off welfare risks; “cage-free” is not a complete welfare assessment by itself.

## Evidence quality
The EFSA opinion synthesizes peer-reviewed and grey literature, expert elicitation, and uncertainty analysis. It is authoritative for the questions it addresses, while also reporting limited evidence for some populations and uncertain relationships among group size, stocking density, and stress.

## Strongest counterargument
Animal-welfare evidence does not itself determine whether eating eggs is morally acceptable. That conclusion also depends on ethical commitments, production system, geography, substitution effects, and how competing welfare outcomes are weighted.

## Confidence assessment
No numeric confidence is assigned. The key epistemic point is that a population-health framing excludes this evidence by construction, not because the welfare considerations were assessed and found wanting.
