# Moderate egg consumption and population-level cardiovascular risk

## Claim
At a population level, moderate egg consumption—roughly up to one egg per day—has not shown a consistent association with higher cardiovascular-disease risk across prospective-cohort meta-analyses, although results vary by population and study design.

## Key facts
- A 2020 BMJ analysis combined three large US cohorts with an updated meta-analysis covering 33 risk estimates and reported no overall association between an additional egg per day and cardiovascular-disease risk.
- The pooled studies were heterogeneous, and associations differed by geographic region.
- American Heart Association guidance emphasizes the overall dietary pattern and the replacement of saturated fat over treating dietary cholesterol as an isolated exposure.

## Evidence quality
The BMJ paper includes large prospective cohorts and a systematic review, but the evidence is observational and vulnerable to residual confounding, dietary-measurement error, and variation in what foods eggs replace or accompany. The AHA advisory is an expert synthesis rather than an independent experimental result.

## Strongest counterargument
Other large observational analyses have reported positive associations between dietary cholesterol or egg intake and cardiovascular outcomes. Aggregating population averages may also conceal meaningful individual response differences and subgroup effects.

## Confidence assessment
No numeric confidence is assigned in this starter baseline. The warranted conclusion is conditional: moderate intake is not consistently associated with higher population-level cardiovascular risk, but this does not establish individual safety or settle questions about other health outcomes.
