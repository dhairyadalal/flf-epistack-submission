# Both founding lineages tie to the Huanan market, and ascertainment-bias checks suggest the spatial clustering is not an artifact

## Claim
Both early SARS-CoV-2 lineages (A and B) are linked to the Huanan Seafood Wholesale Market — lineage B in human cases directly associated with the market, lineage A in a market environmental sample and in early human cases who lived near the market — and dedicated analyses argue that the tight spatial clustering of the earliest December 2019 cases around the market is not an artifact of how cases were found.

> **Important correction to the working summary.** The original framing ("Both lineage A and lineage B were detected in samples directly associated with the market") is only partly correct and must be stated carefully. Lineage B is the one found in the eleven sequenced human genomes *directly associated* with the market. Lineage A was **not** found in those direct human market cases; it was detected in a market **environmental** sample (collected after market closure) and in early human cases who *lived or had recently stayed near* the market. The "both lineages at the market" claim is defensible only if "environmental sample + near-market cases" counts, not if it means "both in human cases directly linked to the market."

## Key facts
- **Lineage B in direct human market cases.** Pekar et al. (2022, *Science*) report that all eleven sequenced genomes from humans *directly associated* with the Huanan market were lineage B (including cases from 24 and 26 December 2019). Lineage B introduction was dated to ~18 November 2019.
- **Lineage A near the market, then in a market environmental sample.** Pekar et al. note the earliest lineage A humans "lived or had recently stayed close to the market" but lacked a documented market link. Liu et al. (2023, *Nature*) released raw sequencing data confirming **lineage A in environmental sample A20** collected at the market (1 January 2020). Débarre (2024, *Virus Evolution*) describes this as independently validating the prediction that "lineage A was indeed in the market."
- **Two separate introductions.** Pekar et al. conclude the two lineages reflect *at least two separate cross-species transmission events*, both placed at or near the market — a pattern they argue is hard to reconcile with a single point-source lab leak.
- **Spatial clustering, including unlinked cases.** Worobey et al. (2022, *Science*) used kernel density estimation on 155 December 2019 cases: the market lies inside the highest-density 1% contour. Crucially, this held even for the 120 cases with *no known link* to the market. Median case-to-market distance was 4.28 km vs 16.11 km for population controls (p < 0.001).
- **Ascertainment-bias checks.** Worobey et al. argue clustering is not a reporting artifact because early cases were identified from clinical signs (not market links or residence), the unlinked cases were *closer* to the market than linked cases (opposite of what bias toward market-linked detection would produce), and the signal survived removing 63% of the nearest cases. Débarre & Worobey (2024, arXiv:2405.08040) reply directly to the bias critique, arguing the unlinked-closer pattern is explained by infection sites not equaling residences plus stochasticity, "without requiring any ascertainment bias."

## Evidence quality
Mixed but mostly high. The genomic and spatial findings come from two peer-reviewed *Science* papers (Pekar 2022; Worobey 2022) by a large overlapping author group, plus a peer-reviewed *Nature* paper (Liu et al. 2023) releasing the underlying market environmental sequencing data. The lineage-A-in-environmental-sample finding is genuinely independent of the original authors (it rests on Chinese CDC data) and is a real strengthening of the case. The ascertainment-bias rebuttal (Débarre & Worobey 2024) is a **preprint reply**, not yet a peer-reviewed article, and the team it defends overlaps with the original authors — so it is not independent.

A real weakness: the two *Science* papers share authors, methods, and a strong prior, so they are not as independent as their separate publication suggests. The lineage-A environmental detection is the most independent and robust single element here.

## Strongest counterargument
There are two serious, *peer-reviewed* rebuttals, which is why this item is not "strong":

1. **The ascertainment-bias critique is published, not fringe.** Weissman (2024, *Journal of the Royal Statistical Society Series A*) argues the clustering can arise from **collider-stratification bias**: if cases were ascertained both by documented market links *and* by proximity, then among observed cases the unlinked ones would paradoxically appear closer to the market — exactly the pattern Worobey reports (unlinked median 4.00 km vs linked 5.74 km, p = 0.029). Under this reading, the spatial signal is partly an artifact of detection, weakening its evidential force. A separate JRSS-A paper (Stoyan & Chiu 2024) also argues the statistics did not *prove* the market was the epicenter. The Débarre & Worobey reply contests Weissman but does not end the dispute.

2. **Environmental positives can reflect human shedding, not animal infection.** Even Débarre (2024, *Virus Evolution*) — sympathetic to natural origin — stresses that market environmental samples were collected *after* extensive human-to-human transmission, so a positive surface (including the lineage-A A20 sample) cannot distinguish animal-shed from human-shed virus, and market-wide correlation analyses suffer from Simpson's-paradox pitfalls. Lab-leak proponents extend this: the market could have been an early super-spreader amplifier seeded by an infected human (including a researcher), and both lineages could cluster there because that is simply where Wuhan's first big transmission event happened, not where spillover originated. The "two introductions" framing has also been disputed (e.g., MDPI/Virus Evolution exchanges over excluded A-B intermediate genomes).

## Confidence assessment
- **Claim confidence:** 0.7 — The component facts are individually well-sourced and largely true *as corrected*: lineage B in direct human market cases (high confidence), lineage A in a market environmental sample and in near-market early cases (high confidence), tight December clustering including unlinked cases (high confidence). The confidence is held below ~0.85 because (a) the original "both lineages in samples directly associated with the market" wording is misleading, and (b) the strongest sub-claim — that there is *no systematic* proximity ascertainment bias — is actively contested in peer-reviewed statistics literature, so "no bias" should be read as "the authors argue bias is not required," not as settled fact.
- **Support strength:** moderate (for natural origin) — IF the clustering is real and unbiased and both lineages genuinely trace to the market, this meaningfully undercuts the "market = mere amplifier" lab-leak story and supports the market as the spillover locus. It is downgraded from "strong" because the bias question is unresolved, the two flagship papers are not independent, and environmental positives are confounded by prior human transmission.
- **Net:** A calibrated reasoner should treat the market's centrality and the two-lineage pattern as solid and modestly favoring natural origin, while recognizing that the "no ascertainment bias" claim is a live, technically contested point rather than a closed one.
