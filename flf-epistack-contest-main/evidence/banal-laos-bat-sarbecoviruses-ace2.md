# BANAL bat sarbecoviruses in Laos: closest known SARS-CoV-2 relatives, with RBDs that bind human ACE2 and infect human cells in nature

## Claim
Wild horseshoe bats (*Rhinolophus* spp.) in karst caves of Feuang district, northern Laos, harbor sarbecoviruses (BANAL-52, BANAL-103, BANAL-236) whose genomes are up to ~96.8% identical to SARS-CoV-2 — the closest known relatives at the time of publication. Their receptor-binding domains (RBDs) are nearly identical to SARS-CoV-2's, bind human ACE2 with affinity comparable to or higher than early SARS-CoV-2, and the viruses enter and replicate in human cells via ACE2 without prior adaptation. This rebuts the early "no wild virus binds human ACE2 this well, so the RBD must be engineered" argument.

## Key facts
- **Closest known relatives.** Temmam et al. (2022, *Nature*) reported BANAL-52 at **96.8% genome-wide nucleotide identity** to SARS-CoV-2 — higher than RaTG13's 96.1%, making it the closest known relative at publication. BANAL-103 and BANAL-236 are also >96% identical.
- **Sampled ~530 km from Wuhan, in a different country.** The bats were sampled in Feuang, Vientiane Province, Laos: BANAL-52 from *R. malayanus*, BANAL-103 from *R. pusillus*, BANAL-236 from *R. marshalli* (Temmam et al. 2022; Institut Pasteur press release 2021).
- **RBD near-identity in the receptor-contacting region.** The BANAL RBDs are very close to SARS-CoV-2's in the receptor-binding motif. The press release and paper note the RBD differs from SARS-CoV-2 at only a handful of contact residues; structural work (e.g., PDB 8UZE; later structural papers) found the BANAL-236 RBD/hACE2 interface within ~2 Å backbone RMSD of the SARS-CoV-2 complex — i.e., essentially superimposable.
- **Binds human ACE2 and enters human cells without adaptation.** Temmam et al. showed BANAL-52/103/236 bind hACE2 and enter/replicate in human Caco-2 and Calu-3 cells expressing physiological ACE2 levels. The press release states their hACE2 affinity is "similar to that of the SARS-CoV-2 virus"; the paper text reports binding "more efficiently than early strains of SARS-CoV-2."
- **Implication for sampling sparseness.** Finding such close relatives in a single, geographically distant, lightly-sampled cave system implies the reservoir is undersampled, so an unsampled near-ancestor circulating in nature is plausible.
- **Major caveat — no furin cleavage site.** All three BANAL viruses **lack the furin cleavage site (FCS)** at the S1/S2 junction that is central to SARS-CoV-2 pathogenicity and human respiratory tropism. None is a direct progenitor; the FCS would have had to arise later (in an intermediate host, by recombination, or after spillover).

## Evidence quality
The core findings are **peer-reviewed and strong**. Temmam et al. 2022 is in *Nature* (Institut Pasteur / Institut Pasteur du Laos / National University of Laos), with isolated, sequenced viruses and functional ACE2/cell-entry assays. The viruses are real isolates, not just sequences. Follow-on peer-reviewed work has independently characterized BANAL-236: an *eBioMedicine* 2024 study (Tamura et al.) and a *Nature Microbiology* 2024 study (Peña-Hernández et al.) both built/used BANAL clones and confirmed ACE2-mediated human-cell entry — so the central virological claim is **replicated across independent labs**.

One source listed in the working brief deserves a flag: arXiv:2112.06141 ("Laotian Bat Sarbecovirus BANAL-236 Uses ACE2 to Infect Cells by an Unknown Mechanism") is **not a neutral structural paper** — it is a single-author preprint by Steven Quay, a prominent lab-leak advocate, arguing the virus's UTRs are "non-canonical" and unprecedented. It is not peer-reviewed and its framing is contested. The genuine structural evidence for RBD/ACE2 near-identity comes from PDB depositions (e.g., 8UZE) and peer-reviewed structural studies, not from that preprint.

## Strongest counterargument
The strongest rebuttal is **not** that the claim is false, but that it proves less than it seems. Two points:

1. **The "supposedly human-optimized" framing is a strawman about the FCS, not the RBD.** Most serious engineering arguments centered on the polybasic furin cleavage site — which BANAL viruses **lack**. So BANAL rebuts the narrower "the RBD couldn't have evolved naturally" claim, but does nothing to explain the FCS, the single most-cited "unusual" feature.

2. **Genetic closeness ≠ pandemic capability.** Peña-Hernández et al. (2024, *Nature Microbiology*) made molecular clones of BANAL-52 and BANAL-236 and found that, although they enter human cells via ACE2, they have **impaired replication in human nasal epithelial cells, reduced pathogenesis in mice, and diminished transmission in hamsters** relative to SARS-CoV-2. Tamura et al. (2024, *eBioMedicine*) similarly found BANAL-20-236 less fusogenic, less pathogenic, and tropic toward intestinal rather than respiratory cells. So the wild BANAL viruses are demonstrably **not** ready-made human respiratory pandemic agents — substantial additional change (notably the FCS and other spike adaptations) was needed. This is fully compatible with natural emergence via an intermediate host, but it blocks the over-strong reading "a pandemic-ready virus already existed in bats."

## Confidence assessment
- **Claim confidence:** 0.95 — The factual claims (closest known relative at ~96.8%; near-identical RBD; binds human ACE2; enters human cells without adaptation; sampled far from Wuhan) are peer-reviewed in *Nature* and independently replicated. The small residual uncertainty is about precise quantitative wording ("similar to" vs "more efficiently than" early SARS-CoV-2 binding) and structural RMSD figures drawn from secondary structural papers.
- **Support strength:** moderate — for **natural origin**. It decisively refutes the specific "no natural virus binds human ACE2 well, so the RBD was engineered" argument and shows the reservoir is undersampled (so an unsampled near-ancestor is plausible). But it does **not** address the furin cleavage site, BANAL viruses are not direct progenitors, and they are not transmission-competent in humans — so it shifts the prior toward natural origin without being decisive.
- **Net:** A calibrated reasoner should treat BANAL as solid evidence that SARS-CoV-2's RBD/ACE2 binding is well within the natural sarbecovirus range and that the bat reservoir is poorly sampled — defusing one engineering argument — while noting it leaves the furin cleavage site question open and does not by itself prove the pandemic strain arose naturally.
