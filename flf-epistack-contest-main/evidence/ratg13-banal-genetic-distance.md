# RaTG13 and BANAL-52 are close relatives but represent decades of divergence from SARS-CoV-2

## Claim
The two closest known relatives of SARS-CoV-2, the bat coronaviruses RaTG13 (~96.1% genome identity) and BANAL-52 (~96.8% identity), each differ from SARS-CoV-2 by roughly 900–1,100 nucleotides across the ~30 kb genome, corresponding to several decades of estimated evolutionary divergence; neither, nor any other known sarbecovirus, carries the furin (S1/S2 polybasic) cleavage site found in SARS-CoV-2.

## Key facts
- **RaTG13 identity:** Zhou et al. (2020, Nature) reported RaTG13, sampled from a *Rhinolophus affinis* bat in a Mojiang (Yunnan) mine in 2013, as ~96.1% identical to SARS-CoV-2 across the whole genome — at the time the single closest known relative. (Note: figures of 96.1%–96.3% appear in different papers depending on alignment method; both are commonly cited.)
- **Magnitude of the gap:** ~96% identity over a ~29,900-nt genome implies on the order of ~1,100 nucleotide differences for RaTG13 (~3.9%) and ~960 for BANAL-52 (~3.2%). The widely used shorthand "900+ nucleotides" is correct as a lower bound and broadly accurate.
- **BANAL viruses:** Temmam et al. (2022, Nature) described BANAL-52, BANAL-103, and BANAL-236 from bats in Feuang, Laos. BANAL-52 and BANAL-103 show ~96.8% genome identity to SARS-CoV-2 and, critically, an receptor-binding domain (RBD) almost identical to SARS-CoV-2's — differing by "only one or two residues."
- **Natural ACE2 binding:** The BANAL viruses bind human ACE2 with affinity similar to (BANAL-236 reportedly higher than) early SARS-CoV-2 and enter human cells via ACE2 — without engineering. This shows the SARS-CoV-2-like RBD pre-exists in wild bats.
- **No furin cleavage site:** Neither RaTG13, BANAL-52, BANAL-103, BANAL-236, nor any other sampled sarbecovirus carries the SARS-CoV-2 S1/S2 furin cleavage motif (RRAR). This is the single most consistent genomic difference between SARS-CoV-2 and its closest known relatives.
- **Divergence time:** Boni et al. (2020, Nature Microbiology) estimated, using recombination-aware molecular clocks, that the SARS-CoV-2 lineage diverged from RaTG13 on the order of ~40–70 years ago (point estimate ~1969 for the RaTG13 split; other reservoir-divergence estimates centered ~1948–1982). This implies no single sampled virus is a "direct backbone" — many decades of unsampled evolution lie between them.

## Evidence quality
High. The identity figures, RBD comparisons, and ACE2 binding come from peer-reviewed papers in *Nature* (Zhou 2020; Temmam 2022) with deposited genome sequences (e.g., RaTG13 GenBank MN996532; BANAL sequences in GISAID/GenBank), and have been independently re-analyzed by many groups. The divergence-time estimates (Boni et al. 2020, *Nature Microbiology*) are model-dependent and carry wide credible intervals, but the qualitative conclusion — decades, not years, of divergence — is robust across methods. The absence of a furin site in all sampled relatives is an uncontested observation. The main genuine uncertainty is in precise nucleotide-difference counts (alignment-dependent) and in absolute divergence dates (clock-rate-dependent).

## Strongest counterargument
The interpretation, not the facts, is contested, and it cuts both ways — which is why this item is neutral.

- **Lab-leak reading:** The WIV held RaTG13 (the closest backbone at the time of the outbreak) and a related virus from the same Mojiang mine, and no sampled natural virus bridges the gap to SARS-CoV-2 — in particular none has the furin cleavage site, the very feature most associated with human transmissibility. Proponents (e.g., Chan & Zhan's furin-site analysis) argue the unique, "out-of-place" furin insertion in an otherwise bat-like backbone is what a synthetic-biology or serial-passage program might add.
- **Natural-origin reading:** Temmam et al. and Garry (2022, PNAS, "SARS-CoV-2 furin cleavage site was not engineered") counter that BANAL viruses demonstrate the hardest-to-explain "human-adapted" feature — a human-ACE2-binding RBD — already exists in nature without any lab contact, and that furin cleavage sites arise naturally in many coronaviruses by insertion/recombination. The decades-long divergence (Boni 2020) directly undercuts the idea that any *known* virus, including RaTG13, was a ready-made backbone.

Net: the same genetic gap is genuinely double-edged.

## Confidence assessment
- **Claim confidence:** 0.95 — The core quantitative facts (≈96–97% identity, ~900+ nt differences, decades of divergence, no furin site in any relative) are well-sourced and uncontested. The small deduction reflects method-dependence in exact nucleotide counts and divergence dates, and the slightly variable 96.1% vs 96.3% RaTG13 figure.
- **Support strength:** weak — As a discriminator between hypotheses it is weak: the facts are fully consistent with both natural spillover (via unsampled intermediates) and a lab origin (via an unrecovered or modified backbone). It constrains but does not resolve.
- **Net:** A calibrated reasoner should treat the RaTG13/BANAL gap as established fact but as near-zero net evidence on origin — it rules out the naïve claim that any sampled virus is a direct ancestor, while neither confirming nor excluding a lab role; the missing furin site in all relatives is the one feature both camps build arguments on.
